A data story about Australian road trips

Australia by Road

Five iconic drives. Five very different climates. This site argues a single idea: when you go matters more than where you go. Some Australian roads are forgiving year-round. Others have a narrow weather window — miss it, and you get a closed road, a heatwave, or a washout.

01 · The scale

NSW collects almost half the country's road-trip spend

Before we meet the five routes, a sense of how big the prize is. Australians spend $110 billion a year on overnight travel — but only three states see most of the money. The same three states that, not coincidentally, have the easiest weather.

03 · At a glance

Distance is only half the planning problem

The bigger question is not how long each road is — it's how many months of the year you can comfortably drive it. A short road with a wide window beats a long road with a closed one. The next chart introduces the metric this whole site is built on.

Distance, days, endpoints

Driving days assume a comfortable ~500 km/day touring pace.

Km per available month

Total distance ÷ ideal-driving months gives the daily reality. The Stuart packs 227 km into every usable month; the Great Ocean Road just 27. Same kind of road trip in name only.

Derived: distance ÷ count of months rated “Ideal” on the drive score.

04 · The argument

When you go matters more than where

This chart is the heart of the site. Each cell is one month on one route, coloured by a drive score — a custom metric combining temperature and rainfall. Green means go; red means avoid. Some routes glow green year-round. Others have a narrow window.

Inputs: Bureau of Meteorology long-term monthly averages for one representative town per route.

How the drive score works

Temp > 33°C→heat penalty
Temp < 18°C→cold penalty
Rain > 75 mm→wet penalty

Score = 100 − total penalties (capped at 0). Thresholds chosen for a comfort-focused holidaymaker, not a hardened outback traveller.

Look at the contrast. Nullarbor & Stuart Highway: green every month. Gibb River Road: half the year unusable. Same continent, different rules.

05 · The temperature behind the score

Stuart Highway swings 16°C across the year — Gibb barely moves

Five small charts, one per route, showing the average monthly maximum temperature at a representative town. Each point is coloured by its drive rating. Watch which routes swing widely (the Stuart: 36°C summer, 20°C winter) and which barely change (the Gibb is hot regardless of when you go).

Source: Bureau of Meteorology long-term averages. Stations: Derby (WA), Alice Springs (NT), Coffs Harbour (NSW), Apollo Bay (VIC), Nullarbor (SA).

06 · The rain behind the score

The Pacific Highway is actually wetter than the Gibb — on average

Heat is half the story; rain is the other. The Gibb's rain is a violent three-month wet season that closes the road entirely. The east coast spreads its rainfall across the year — less dramatic, but it adds up to more millimetres than the Gibb sees most years.

Same five stations as Section 05. Note how Nullarbor and Stuart sit dry all year.

07 · The geography of weather

Cross more latitude, meet more weather

Why does the Stuart Highway have such extreme seasonality, while the Great Ocean Road barely budges? The answer is mostly geographic. A route's latitude span determines how many climate zones it passes through.

Dashed line marks the Tropic of Capricorn. Routes that cross it pass between tropical and temperate zones.

Geography → Climate

More latitude crossed = more climate zones = wider weather swings.

The two extremes

Stuart Highway20.0°
Great Ocean Road0.4°

The Stuart crosses 50 times more latitude than the Great Ocean Road. That's why one has wild seasonality and the other doesn't.

08 · A climate fingerprint

Every route's year, drawn as a journey

One last way to see it. Each route's twelve months are connected as a loop on temperature and rainfall. The shape is the climate. Three signatures jump out:

Gibb's vertical drop

The green loop falls almost straight down — the wet season ending, rainfall plummeting from 180 mm in January to under 5 mm by June. Same temperature, totally different road.

Pacific's high arc

The pink curve sits up in the rainfall axis all year. Endless summer rain. No dramatic seasons — just a long wet stretch the east coast has learned to live with.

Nullarbor barely moves

The yellow line is the tightest cluster on the chart. The driest, mildest climate of any iconic route. That's why it's driveable every month of the year.

09 · A year on one road

The Gibb River Road has the narrowest window in Australia

The Gibb is the country's most seasonally extreme drive. Its year reads like a clock face. Six months are open, six are not.

Each wedge is one month. The number inside each wedge is the average rainfall in millimetres.

The wet season

January peaks at 182 mm of rain. The road is officially closed; rivers cross the track. Unusable for travellers.

The dry season

May to September averages under 5 mm per month. Hot and dry. Ideal driving conditions.

The window

Six months out of twelve. Miss your window and you wait a full year for the next.

The Gibb isn't a "drive when you can" road. It's a "drive exactly now" road.

10 · The takeaway

So when do you go?

Not all road trips play by the same rules. Pick the row that matches your constraint and start there.

If you're flexible on dates

Stuart Highway or Nullarbor

Both will accept you almost any month. The Stuart shines in winter; a summer crossing is doable if you're prepared. The Nullarbor is the steadiest road of all five.

If you can only travel in summer

Great Ocean Road

Cool ocean breeze, peak season, dry-ish weather. Avoid the Gibb (closed) and the Pacific Highway (wettest months of the year). Stuart is doable but hot.

If you can only travel in winter

Pacific Highway or Gibb

Both shine in the dry, cool months. The Pacific is at its driest; the Gibb is at its prime. The Great Ocean Road gets a touch cold and damp.

If you want the Gibb specifically

May to September only

The narrowest window of any iconic Australian drive. The Gibb has one open season per year. Miss it and you wait twelve months for the next.
